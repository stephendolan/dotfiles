# Bad Data Taxonomy — Extended Pattern Library

Use this when scanning a transcript or notes to bucket each customer utterance. Most "warm" interview output is bad data wearing validation's clothes.

## Compliments

**Pattern markers:**
- "That's a great idea."
- "I love this."
- "You're really onto something."
- "This is exactly what we need." *(without follow-up specificity)*
- "I can see this being huge."
- "Smart approach."
- Any reaction to *your idea* rather than *their situation*

**Why it's poison:** Costs nothing to give. Social politeness. The compliment is about you (or about being nice to you), not about whether the problem is real or whether they'd act.

**Redirect techniques:**
- Acknowledge briefly, then anchor: *"Glad it resonates. Tell me about the last time this came up for you — what did you do?"*
- Convert to a commitment: *"Would you want to be one of our first 10 users? We're taking deposits."*
- If they keep complimenting without anchoring: politely note you're trying to learn, not pitch, and re-ask about their world.

**In a transcript, never log a compliment as a "positive signal."** It's neutral at best, misleading at worst.

## Fluff — Generic Claims

**Pattern markers:**
- "I always..."
- "I usually..."
- "I never..."
- "We tend to..."
- "Everyone in our industry..."
- "Most people I know..."
- "It's pretty common to..."

**Why it's poison:** Describes an idealized or stereotyped version of behavior, not a specific instance. The story they tell themselves about what they do is usually different from what they actually did last Tuesday.

**Redirect techniques:**
- *"Can you give me a specific example? When was the last time?"*
- *"Talk me through the most recent instance."*
- *"You said 'usually' — what about the last time? Was that typical?"*

## Fluff — Hypothetical Maybes

**Pattern markers:**
- "I might..."
- "I could see myself..."
- "It depends on..."
- "If the right thing came along..."
- "I'd be open to..."
- "Possibly..."

**Why it's poison:** Pure speculation about a future self. The future self is always more disciplined, organized, and willing to pay than the actual self.

**Redirect techniques:**
- *"What have you done in that situation so far?"*
- *"When you faced something similar last time, what did you actually do?"*
- *"What would have to change for you to actually do that?"*

## Fluff — Future-Tense Promises

**Pattern markers:**
- "I would definitely buy that."
- "I'll start using this."
- "Once it exists, I'm in."
- "Sign me up when you launch."
- "I'd love to try it."

**Why it's poison:** The most dangerous category — sounds like real validation. But it costs nothing to verbally agree to a future action.

**Redirect technique — convert to commitment:**
- *"Want to pre-order? We're taking deposits."*
- *"We're booking pilot slots — want one?"*
- *"Can I send a contract for early access?"*
- *"Can you intro me to your head of X so we can pilot this with your team?"*

If they balk at the commitment, the future promise was fluff. If they accept, you have real signal.

**Heuristic: People stop lying when you ask them for money.**

## Feature Requests / Ideas

**Pattern markers:**
- "You should add..."
- "If it had X, I'd use it."
- "What would be really useful is..."
- "Have you thought about..."
- "It needs..."
- "I wish it could..."

**Why it's poison:** A feature request is a *symptom*. It tells you the customer noticed something missing, but it doesn't tell you (a) whether the underlying problem matters enough to pay for, (b) whether the proposed feature actually solves it, or (c) whether you'd be the right one to build it.

**The diagnostic principle:** *You own the solution; they own the problem.*

**Redirect techniques — diagnose the motivation:**
- *"Why do you want that?"*
- *"What would that enable you to do?"*
- *"How are you managing without it right now?"*
- *"How would that fit into your current workflow?"*
- *"What would change for you if you had it?"*

The answer to those questions is the real data. The original feature request is just the doorway.

## Avoidance Signals (Negative Data)

**Pattern markers:**
- Won't commit to a follow-up call
- Won't intro you to anyone, even when asked
- "Check back in a few months."
- "Send me an email and I'll get back to you." *(without scheduling)*
- "Let me know how it goes."
- Repeated rescheduling without engagement

**Why it matters:** Polite-sounding non-commitment is the loudest negative signal you'll get. People who care will give you time, intros, or money. People who don't will give you warmth and avoidance.

**How to read it:** Update toward "this person isn't a real prospect" and don't pursue them further. Spend your time with people who care.

## Emotion Signals (Where to Lean In)

Not bad data — these are where the gold lives. Mark them in transcripts.

**Pattern markers:**
- Visible frustration ("ugh," sighs, "honestly...")
- Resignation ("we just live with it")
- Excitement that's specific to their experience (not your idea)
- Strong language ("hate," "drives me crazy," "nightmare")
- Stories that come unprompted

**Dig questions for these moments:**
- *"Tell me more about that."*
- *"That seems to really bug you — I bet there's a story here."*
- *"Why hasn't this been fixed already?"*
- *"What makes it so awful?"*
- *"What would happen if you couldn't fix this?"*

## Transcript-Scanning Heuristic

Mark every customer utterance with one of these tags:

- 🥇 **Gold** — specific past behavior, concrete constraint, money or time spent, current workaround described in detail
- 💬 **Compliment** — about you or your idea, not their situation
- 🌫️ **Fluff** — generic, hypothetical, or future-tense
- 💡 **Feature request** — note it, but don't act on it; capture the motivation behind it instead
- 🔥 **Emotion signal** — strong reaction that needed a follow-up
- ✋ **Avoidance** — declined, deflected, or non-committed
- 💰 **Commitment** — gave time, reputation, or money

Then ask: **what's the gold-to-fluff ratio?** A call that's 80% fluff is a fluff call regardless of how it felt at the time.
