# Commitment and Advancement

A meeting that doesn't result in a defined next step was a waste of time, no matter how warm it felt. Push for a concrete commitment in one of three escalating currencies.

**Definition:** Commitment is what someone gives up *that they value*. Time, reputation, or money. Words don't count.

**Definition:** Advancement is moving them to the next concrete step in your funnel — closer to a real purchase decision.

In practice these are the same thing: if you're getting one, you're usually getting the other. If you're getting neither, the meeting failed.

## Currency 1: Time (lightest)

Someone unwilling to give you more time is almost certainly not a real prospect. Time commitments include:

- **Specific next meeting** with a defined goal and agenda *(not "let's stay in touch")*
- Agreement to review wireframes, a prototype, or a doc
- Committing to test the product for a defined period
- Going out of their way — travel, schedule rearrangement
- Sending you something they have to make (an internal doc, a workflow description)

**Time-commitment ask formulas:**
- *"Could we book 30 minutes next week to walk through wireframes? I want your reaction before we build."*
- *"Would you be willing to test a prototype for a week and give us feedback?"*
- *"I'd love to come watch you do this work for an hour. When's a good time?"*

## Currency 2: Reputation (medium)

They put their name behind you. This costs them socially if you fail, so it's a real signal.

- Introduction to a peer, colleague, or domain expert
- Introduction to a decision-maker / their boss / leadership
- Public testimonial or case study
- Social media mention or public endorsement
- Recommending you to someone who matters to them

**Reputation-commitment ask formulas:**
- *"Who else on your team should I talk to? Could you introduce me?"*
- *"Is there anyone else in your network dealing with this? Mind making an intro?"*
- *"Would you be willing to write up your experience as a case study once we're further along?"*

## Currency 3: Money (heaviest)

The closest proxy for real purchase intent. The point isn't the dollar amount — it's the act of giving you something they'd otherwise keep.

- Letter of intent (LOI) — non-binding but written commitment
- Pre-order
- Deposit (even small)
- Pilot contract with a fee
- Actual purchase of an early/prototype version

**Money-commitment ask formulas:**
- *"We're taking deposits for early access. Want to lock in a slot?"*
- *"Pilots are $X for 90 days. Want to book one?"*
- *"Can we do a paid pilot — $X for the first month while we tune it for you?"*
- *"Could you put down a refundable deposit so I can prioritize your needs in the build?"*

**Heuristic:** People stop lying when you ask them for money. A verbal "I'd buy this" is fluff. A signed LOI or a credit card deposit is signal.

## Ladder of Commitment

When you don't know what to ask for, climb the ladder until they say yes:

1. Will you let me watch you do this work?
2. Will you give me 30 more minutes next week to react to a wireframe?
3. Will you intro me to one other person who has this problem?
4. Will you intro me to your boss / the buyer?
5. Will you sign an LOI?
6. Will you put down a deposit?
7. Will you pay for a pilot?

The point at which they stop saying yes is your real position in their priority stack.

## Failed-Meeting Tells

Mark these as warning signs, not validation:

- "Let me know when you launch."
- "Send me more info."
- "Definitely interested — keep me posted."
- "I'd use that for sure." *(with no defined next step)*
- "Sounds great." *(period)*
- "Let's stay in touch."

If the call ends with one of these and no concrete next action, the call failed regardless of how warm the conversation felt. Treat it as a soft no.

## Successful-Meeting Tells

These are the words you actually want to hear:

- "Can I sign up?"
- "How do I get access?"
- "Can I introduce you to [decision-maker]?"
- "What are next steps?"
- "When can you meet with our team?"
- "Can I buy the prototype?"
- "Send me the contract."

When you hear these, the prospect is real and you should immediately convert the energy into a specific next action.

## How to Ask Without Being Pushy

1. **Decide before the meeting** what commitment ask you'll make. Don't improvise.
2. **State the ask explicitly** before the call ends. Vague hints don't count.
3. **Accept the answer gracefully.** Clarity is the goal, not persuasion. A clear "no" is better than a warm "maybe."
4. **If they decline, ask what would have to be true for that to change.** This sometimes surfaces the real blocker (budget cycle, decision authority, missing feature, wrong segment).

**Rule:** If you're afraid to ask for a commitment because you think they'll say no — that's exactly why you need to ask. The fear is the data.

## Reviewer Checklist (when auditing a call guide)

When reviewing someone's call guide, scan for:

- ☐ Is there an explicit commitment ask anywhere in the guide?
- ☐ Which currency does it ask for (time / reputation / money)?
- ☐ Is the ask specific (defined next meeting, named intro, dollar amount), or vague ("stay in touch")?
- ☐ If the guide is for a discovery call, is the ask appropriately light (time)? If it's a validation call, is it appropriately heavy (money)?
- ☐ Does the guide give the prospect a clear chance to *reject* the ask, or does it set up the conversation so a polite "sounds good" feels like success?

A call guide without a commitment ask is a call guide that will end in fluff.
