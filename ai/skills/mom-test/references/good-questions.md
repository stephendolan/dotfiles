# Good Question Library

Question patterns Fitzpatrick endorses, organized by what each unlocks. Use these as raw material when proposing replacements during pre-call review.

## Reveal actual workflow (anchored past behavior)

- "Walk me through the last time [X happened]."
- "Talk me through what you did this morning when [X]."
- "What was the most recent instance? How did it go?"
- "Tell me about a recent project where this came up."
- "What did you try? How did that work?"

These force specificity. They turn "I usually do X" into "Last Tuesday I did X and it went sideways because..."

## Reveal motivation (separate vitamin from painkiller)

- "Why do you bother [doing this thing]?"
- "What are the implications when [X] happens?"
- "What would happen if you couldn't fix this?"
- "What's the cost of getting this wrong?"
- "How does this rank against everything else on your plate this quarter?"

These distinguish problems people *complain* about from problems people *act* on.

## Reveal existing solutions (your real competition)

- "How are you dealing with it now?"
- "What else have you tried to solve this?"
- "What tools are you currently using? What do you like and not like about them?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand, what would change about your current setup?" *(use sparingly — borderline hypothetical, but useful when paired with "and what have you done about it so far?")*

**Heuristic:** If they haven't already tried to solve it, they won't buy your solution. The existence of a current workaround is itself signal that the problem matters.

## Reveal economics (especially B2B)

- "Where does the money come from?"
- "Who has authority to sign off on a purchase like this?"
- "What's your current spend on [adjacent category]?"
- "Who has been pushing for this internally?"
- "What's the budget cycle look like?"
- "Who else needs to be in the room for this decision?"

## Reveal network (and indirectly, conviction)

- "Who else should I talk to about this?"
- "Do you know anyone else dealing with this?"
- "Would you be willing to introduce me?"

**The signal isn't in the names — it's in the willingness.** People who genuinely care will connect you to others. Hesitation or "I'd have to think about it" is a soft no on the underlying interest.

## Surface blind spots

- "Is there anything I should have asked?"
- "What am I missing?"
- "What didn't I cover that's important?"
- "What would you ask if you were me?"

Best at the end of a call. Often produces the most useful single quote.

## Dig into emotion (when you spot a reaction)

- "Tell me more about that."
- "That seems to really bug you — I bet there's a story here."
- "Why hasn't this been fixed already?"
- "What makes it so [frustrating/annoying/awful]?"
- "What did you do when that happened?"

Strong emotion is where priority lives. Lean in.

## Validation-mode questions (when you're showing something)

These are *not* discovery questions. Use them only after the discovery section, when you've earned the right to show your idea:

- "What concerns you about this?" *(not "what do you like" — invites criticism, which is more useful)*
- "What would you have to give up to have this?"
- "What would need to be true for this to be a no-brainer?"
- "Want to be one of our first [N] users? We're booking pilots."
- "Can I send you a contract / take a deposit / book the kickoff?"

The goal of validation isn't agreement — it's a concrete commitment or a concrete reason it won't happen.

## The "terrifying question" check

Before finalizing a call guide, ask yourself: which of these questions am I most afraid to ask? If the answer is "none of them," you don't have a real discovery call — you have a comfort call. Add the scary question.

Examples of terrifying questions:
- "When you described this problem earlier — was it actually a top-3 priority for you, or somewhere lower?"
- "If we built this exactly as I described and put it in front of you tomorrow, would you actually pay for it? What would the price need to be?"
- "Of the meetings you had this week, how many would have been better if an agent participated? Be honest — could be zero."

The right terrifying question is specific, anchored to their actual life, and could plausibly disconfirm your idea.

## Anti-question Catalog (avoid these)

Reject any of these in a call guide. They are reliably fluff-magnets:

| Bad question | Why |
|---|---|
| "Do you think this is a good idea?" | Markets decide, not individuals. |
| "Would you buy this?" | Hypothetical future. |
| "How much would you pay?" | Hypothetical pricing. |
| "What's your dream product?" | Wishlist, not problem severity. |
| "Do you care about [security/quality/X]?" | Nobody says no. |
| "Doesn't that sound useful?" | Leading. |
| "Would you find this valuable?" | Pure validation-seeking. |
| "Would you want either of those things?" | Hypothetical preference between two future scenarios. |

If a call guide contains any of these, propose a Mom-Test-compliant replacement that targets the same underlying learning by anchoring to past behavior or asking for commitment.
